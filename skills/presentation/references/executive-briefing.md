# Executive Briefing Template

## When to Use
- C-suite or VP-level status update (15 minutes or less)
- Escalation requiring an immediate decision
- Board meeting pre-read or summary
- Quarterly business review contribution
- Crisis or incident communication to leadership

## The Executive Mindset

Executives operate under extreme time pressure. They:
- Scan, not read — your first sentence determines if they engage
- Think in portfolios — your project is one of 30+ they track
- Want the answer first, rationale second, details never (unless they ask)
- Judge your judgment — HOW you present signals your competence
- Hate surprises — flag bad news early with a mitigation plan attached

## The BLUF Framework (Bottom Line Up Front)

Every executive briefing follows BLUF:

```
BOTTOM LINE: [What happened / what you recommend / what you need]
CONTEXT:     [2-3 sentences of essential background]
DETAILS:     [Only if asked — have them ready]
```

## Slide Structure

### Slide 1: Title + Bottom Line
- Title: Project/Initiative name
- Subtitle: THE one-sentence bottom line
  - Status update: "Project Alpha is on track for Q3 launch with one risk to flag"
  - Decision needed: "We recommend Option B — $800K, ships 6 weeks faster"
  - Escalation: "Payment processing is down for EU customers — here's our recovery plan"
- **Speaker Notes**: Deliver the bottom line in the first 15 seconds. Then pause. Let them react.

### Slide 2: Status Dashboard (for status updates)
- Traffic light format:

| Area          | Status | Note                          |
|---------------|--------|-------------------------------|
| Timeline      | Green  | On track for June 15          |
| Budget        | Yellow | 8% over — mitigation in place |
| Quality       | Green  | 0 P1 bugs in staging          |
| Risk          | Yellow | Vendor dependency — see slide 3|

- Or: Metric-based dashboard with actuals vs. targets
- **Rule**: If everything is green, this meeting should be an email. Highlight what needs attention.

### Slide 2 (alt): The Recommendation (for decisions)
- **Recommendation**: One sentence
- **Impact**: One number (revenue, cost, risk reduction, time saved)
- **Investment**: One number (cost, resources, time)
- **Timeline**: One date (when it ships / when you need the decision by)

### Slide 3: The One Thing That Needs Attention
- The risk, blocker, or decision that brought you into the room
- State it plainly: what it is, what happens if unaddressed, what you're doing about it
- Include your recommendation (don't just present the problem)
- **Speaker Notes**: Executives respect people who bring solutions, not just problems

### Slide 4: Key Metrics / Evidence
- Maximum 4 metrics
- Each metric: current value, target, trend direction (arrow up/down/flat)
- If a metric is off-track, the mitigation is on the same slide (not "we'll address later")

### Slide 5: Next Steps + The Ask
- 2-3 next steps with owners and dates
- The specific decision or action you need from THEM
- When you need it by
- What you'll report back at the next checkpoint

### Appendix (Have Ready, Don't Present)
- Detailed timeline
- Full financial breakdown
- Technical details
- Team roster and allocation
- Competitive context
- Historical trend data

## Time Budgeting

| Slot Length | Presenting | Discussion | Buffer |
|------------|-----------|------------|--------|
| 5 min      | 2 min     | 2 min      | 1 min  |
| 15 min     | 5 min     | 8 min      | 2 min  |
| 30 min     | 10 min    | 15 min     | 5 min  |

**The discussion IS the meeting.** Your slides set up the conversation. Budget more time for their questions than for your talking.

## Question Preparation

Before any executive briefing, prepare answers for:

1. **"What's the worst case?"** — Have a specific scenario and your response plan
2. **"How does this compare to [competitor/last quarter/the plan]?"** — Have the benchmark
3. **"What do you need from me?"** — Always have a specific ask, even if it's "just awareness"
4. **"What happens if we do nothing?"** — Quantify the cost of inaction
5. **"When will we know if this is working?"** — Have the first measurable checkpoint

## Red/Yellow/Green Definitions

Standardize so executives can scan instantly:

| Color  | Meaning                                           |
|--------|---------------------------------------------------|
| Green  | On track. No action needed.                       |
| Yellow | At risk. Mitigation in place. Monitoring closely. |
| Red    | Off track. Need decision or help.                 |
| Grey   | Not started or not applicable this period.        |

**Rule**: Never present all-green. If everything is truly green, compress to one status line and give time back. If you're hiding yellows as greens, you'll lose trust when reality catches up.

## Escalation-Specific Guidance

When the briefing IS an escalation:

1. **Lead with impact**: "X customers are affected" not "System Y is experiencing issues"
2. **State what you know AND what you don't**: Uncertainty is fine; being unaware is not
3. **Show the timeline**: When it started, when you found out, what you've done, when it'll be resolved
4. **Own it**: "We missed this because..." builds more trust than "It was caused by..."
5. **Close with prevention**: "Here's what we're doing so this doesn't happen again"

## Tone Rules

- **Crisp**: Short sentences. Active voice. No hedging.
- **Confident**: "We recommend" not "We think" or "We suggest"
- **Honest**: Flag risks early. Execs forgive problems; they don't forgive surprises.
- **Respectful of time**: If you can say it in 5 slides, don't use 10.
- **Prepared**: Every claim should have a backup slide. If they ask "how do you know?", you show the data.
