# Stakeholder Pitch Template

## When to Use
- Seeking approval or funding for an initiative
- Proposing a new product, feature, or strategic direction
- Escalating a decision that needs executive sponsorship
- Making a business case for technical investment

## The Persuasion Framework

Every pitch answers these 4 questions IN THIS ORDER:

1. **Why should you care?** (Stakes + relevance to their priorities)
2. **What do we propose?** (The recommendation — crisp, specific)
3. **Why will it work?** (Evidence, not opinion)
4. **What do you need to do?** (The ask — specific action, amount, timeline)

## Slide Structure

### Slide 1: The Headline Ask
- Title IS the ask: "Approve $1.5M to Launch Patient Portal by Q3"
- Subtitle: One sentence connecting to a strategic priority they own
- **Speaker Notes**: You have 60 seconds of goodwill. Use them on the conclusion.

### Slide 2: Executive Summary
- **The Opportunity**: 1 sentence on what's possible
- **The Recommendation**: 1 sentence on what to do
- **The Impact**: 1 sentence with a number (revenue, cost savings, risk reduction)
- **The Ask**: 1 sentence on what you need from them specifically

### Slide 3: Why Now — The Burning Platform
- What's changing in the market, regulation, or customer behavior?
- What's the cost of inaction? (frame as loss, not missed opportunity)
- Use a concrete number or date that creates urgency
- **Anti-pattern**: Don't manufacture urgency. Real stakes are persuasive; fake ones destroy credibility.

### Slide 4: The Proposal (What We Recommend)
- State the recommendation in one sentence
- 3-4 key elements of the approach
- Frame as the BEST option, not the ONLY option (shows you've thought broadly)

### Slide 5: Options Considered
- Table: Option | Pros | Cons | Recommendation
- Include "Do Nothing" as an explicit option with its consequences
- Maximum 3-4 options — more than that signals you haven't decided
- Bold or highlight the recommended option

### Slide 6: Evidence This Will Work
- Customer data, market research, pilot results, benchmarks, competitor analysis
- Each piece of evidence tied to a specific claim
- "70% of patients in our pilot used the portal weekly" > "We believe patients will use it"
- Include source and date for each data point

### Slide 7: Financial Impact
- Table or simple chart: Investment | Year 1 Return | Year 2 Return | Year 3 Return
- Include both costs and benefits
- Show breakeven point
- **Speaker Notes**: Be ready to defend every number. If you can't, use a range.

### Slide 8: Risk and Mitigation
- 3-4 risks maximum — the ones THEY will worry about
- For each: what we'll do to prevent it, and what we'll do if it happens anyway
- Include one risk you've already mitigated (builds confidence)

### Slide 9: Implementation Timeline
- Phase-based, not task-based
- Each phase: what ships, what it proves, decision gate to continue
- Call out where their input/approval is needed
- Show quick wins in Phase 1 (proves momentum)

### Slide 10: The Ask (Repeated, Specific)
- Restate exactly what you need
- Include amount, timeline, resources, and decision deadline
- Frame what happens next if they say yes (immediate next step)
- Frame what happens if decision is delayed (cost of delay)

### Slide 11: Next Steps + Owners + Dates
- First 3-5 actions after approval
- Named owners (including their reports, not just your team)
- First checkpoint date

### Appendix
- Detailed financial model
- Market research summary
- Technical feasibility assessment
- Competitive landscape
- Team and resource plan

## Persuasion Techniques

### Anchoring
- Present the large number first (total market opportunity), then the ask feels small
- "This is a $50M market. We need $1.5M to capture 10% of it."

### Loss Aversion
- Frame inaction as a loss, not just a missed gain
- "Every quarter we delay costs us $200K in competitive ground"

### Social Proof
- Reference competitors, industry leaders, or internal precedents
- "Three of our top-5 competitors launched this capability in the last 12 months"

### Commitment Consistency
- Tie the ask to decisions they've ALREADY made
- "This aligns with the digital-first strategy the board approved in January"

## Tone Rules

- Confident, not arrogant: "We recommend" not "We believe" or "We think"
- Specific, not vague: "$1.5M over 18 months" not "significant investment"
- Respectful of their time: every slide earns the next one
- Prepared for pushback: "If your concern is X, here's how we address it"
